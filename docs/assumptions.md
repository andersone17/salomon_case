# **Case Study Assumptions**

## **1. Purpose**

This document records the assumptions used to construct and evaluate the fictional Salomon Vestal Pro campaign.

These assumptions are intended to make the synthetic analysis internally consistent and transparent.

---

## **2. General Assumptions**

- The campaign and all associated data are fictional.
- The analysis represents Salomon North America eCommerce.
- Currency is reported in U.S. dollars.
- Dates represent calendar dates in 2026.
- The generated data is intended for interview demonstration purposes only.
- Synthetic data should resemble realistic commercial data but should not be interpreted as actual Salomon performance.
- All customer identifiers are synthetic and anonymized.
- Tax and shipping revenue are excluded from product net revenue unless otherwise noted.

---

## **3. Campaign Timing Assumptions**

| Period | Start Date | End Date |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-Campaign Baseline | 2026-03-01 | 2026-05-31 |
| Active Campaign | 2026-06-01 | 2026-06-20 |
| Post-Campaign Period | 2026-06-21 | 2026-07-20 |

The active campaign lasts 20 days.

The pre-campaign period is used to estimate expected performance without the campaign.

The post-campaign period is used to evaluate:

- Demand persistence
- Delayed purchases
- Returns
- Repeat purchases
- Continued halo effects
- Consumer database value

---

## **4. Product Assumptions**

- The Vestal Pro is treated as a newly launched trail-running franchise.
- The product is sold at or near full price during the campaign.
- The Vestal Pro has men's and women's variants.
- Each gender variant includes multiple sizes and colorways.
- Specific core sizes generate a disproportionate share of demand.
- Hero colorways receive greater campaign exposure and demand.
- Existing footwear franchises remain available throughout the campaign, although availability varies by product and size.
- The Vestal Pro competes most directly with Ultra Glide, followed by Genesis.
- Ultra Glide should show the largest estimated cannibalization during the campaign.
- Highly specialized footwear such as Speedcross experiences less cannibalization.
- Apparel, socks, hydration products, and trail accessories are considered adjacent categories for halo analysis.

---

## **5. Pricing and Margin Assumptions**

Illustrative price points may include:

| Product Category | Approximate Price Range |
|---|---|
| Vestal Pro | $150 to $180 |
| Existing Trail Footwear | $130 to $200 |
| Trail Apparel | $40 to $160 |
| Trail Accessories | $15 to $90 |
| Hydration Products | $30 to $180 |

Additional assumptions:

- The Vestal Pro launches without a major promotional discount.
- Existing footwear may contain a small amount of markdown activity.
- Accessories and apparel may have higher percentage margins than footwear.
- Returns reduce net revenue and gross margin.
- Campaign marketing spend is deducted when estimating contribution margin.

---

## **6. Customer Assumptions**

- A new customer has no completed Salomon North America eCommerce purchase before their first campaign-period transaction.
- An existing customer has at least one prior completed purchase.
- CRM members may include both customers and non-purchasing subscribers.
- Paid social produces the highest share of new visitors and new customers.
- CRM produces the highest share of existing-customer orders.
- Existing customers generally have higher conversion rates and average order values.
- New customers generally have lower first-order average order values.
- New Vestal Pro customers have relatively strong email and SMS opt-in rates.
- The observation window is too short to measure complete customer lifetime value.
- Repeat-purchase behavior is treated as an early indicator of future customer value.

---

## **7. Channel Assumptions**

### **7.1 CRM**

- CRM includes email and SMS.
- CRM media cost is substantially lower than paid-media cost.
- CRM reporting uses attributed revenue rather than fully incremental revenue.
- CRM audiences have higher existing purchase intent.
- Email reaches a larger audience than SMS.
- SMS produces higher urgency and conversion but lower total revenue.

### **7.2 Paid Social**

- Paid social is primarily used for prospecting, awareness, and new-customer acquisition.
- Paid social has a higher customer-acquisition cost than CRM.
- Paid social contributes to assisted conversions and branded search demand.
- Last-click reporting understates some paid-social influence.

### **7.3 Paid Search**

- Paid search includes branded and non-branded search.
- Branded paid search receives some credit for demand created by other channels.
- Paid search generally has stronger last-click efficiency than paid social.
- Paid-search performance declines when product availability becomes constrained.

### **7.4 Affiliate Marketing**

- Affiliate marketing includes product-review publishers, content sites, and promotional partners.
- Affiliate-reported revenue may include some customers who would have purchased without the affiliate referral.
- Affiliate performance is meaningful but smaller than paid search, paid social, or CRM.

### **7.5 Organic and Direct Traffic**

- Organic and direct traffic increase partly because of campaign awareness.
- Organic and direct channels do not have directly assigned campaign spend.
- Some campaign impact is therefore reflected outside paid-channel attribution.

---

## **8. Attribution Assumptions**

- Channel-attributed revenue is not treated as equivalent to incremental revenue.
- Multiple channels may influence the same customer journey.
- Last-click attribution may over-credit paid search, direct, or affiliate channels.
- CRM audiences may have higher baseline purchase intent than non-CRM audiences.
- Paid social may be under-credited because it often appears earlier in the consumer journey.
- The synthetic data does not provide a perfect multi-touch attribution solution.
- The executive analysis should emphasize business incrementality over reported attribution alone.

---

## **9. Counterfactual Assumptions**

- The pre-campaign period contains sufficient information to estimate an expected no-campaign baseline.
- Expected performance depends on seasonality, traffic, inventory, promotions, product mix, and channel activity.
- No counterfactual model can perfectly observe what would have happened without the campaign.
- Counterfactual results should be presented as estimates with an uncertainty range.
- The campaign effect should remain positive across reasonable alternative specifications.
- Model fit alone should not determine whether the counterfactual is credible.
- Business plausibility and pre-period validation are required.

---

## **10. Cannibalization Assumptions**

- Some Vestal Pro customers would have purchased another Salomon trail shoe if the Vestal Pro had not launched.
- Cannibalization is strongest among products with similar use cases and consumer positioning.
- Cannibalization should be estimated after accounting for:
  - Inventory
  - Promotions
  - Seasonality
  - Existing product trends
  - Category traffic
  - Product lifecycle
- A reduction in existing-franchise sales should not automatically be classified as cannibalization.
- Cannibalization may still be strategically acceptable when:
  - The new product has a higher margin.
  - The new product attracts new customers.
  - The existing product is late in its lifecycle.
  - The launch strengthens Salomon's competitive positioning.

---

## **11. Halo-Effect Assumptions**

- Campaign exposure may increase demand for adjacent products.
- Halo effects may occur within the original transaction or in a later transaction.
- Attach-rate growth is treated as one indicator of halo demand.
- Adjacent-category sales growth must be compared against an expected baseline.
- Not all adjacent-category growth should be attributed to the campaign.
- A positive halo effect should remain smaller than direct Vestal Pro revenue.

---

## **12. Inventory Assumptions**

- Inventory is healthy at the beginning of the launch.
- Core sizes become constrained during the campaign peak.
- Fringe sizes remain available for longer.
- Inventory shortages reduce conversion.
- Some customers substitute into other Salomon footwear.
- Some customers leave the site without purchasing.
- Paid-media campaigns do not immediately adjust to every inventory change.
- Available-to-sell inventory is measured at the end of each reporting day unless otherwise specified.
- Units sold cannot exceed the inventory available for sale.
- Inventory receipts may occur during the campaign but should not fully eliminate shortages.

---

## **13. Returns Assumptions**

- Returns occur after a delay from the original transaction.
- The post-campaign period captures only part of the final return volume.
- Vestal Pro return rates are modestly higher than the existing trail-footwear average.
- Sizing and fit are the most common return reasons.
- Product-defect returns remain uncommon.
- Net revenue should reflect known returns within the observation window.
- A final mature return rate may need to be estimated because of return timing.

---

## **14. Consumer Services Assumptions**

- Consumer Services contacts increase during the campaign because of higher order and traffic volume.
- Sizing, product comparison, and inventory questions increase more quickly than total order volume.
- Consumer Services contacts can signal site-content or product-experience issues.
- Contact volume alone does not indicate a negative campaign outcome.
- Contact rates should be normalized by orders, customers, or sessions.
- One order or customer may generate multiple contacts.

---

## **15. Site Analytics Assumptions**

- Sessions, users, product views, and conversion events are synthetic approximations of standard eCommerce analytics reporting.
- Minor tracking loss or measurement inconsistency may exist.
- Mobile traffic represents the majority of visits.
- Desktop conversion is generally higher than mobile conversion.
- Product-comparison and sizing content improve conversion among engaged visitors.
- Product availability affects conversion independently of content engagement.
- A visitor may interact with multiple campaign pages before purchasing.

---

## **16. Data Quality Assumptions**

The generated data should be analytically usable but not unrealistically perfect.

Possible limitations may include:

- Minor missing values in non-critical descriptive fields
- Delayed channel reporting
- Differences between channel-attributed orders and eCommerce order totals
- Incomplete cross-device identity resolution
- Incomplete customer history
- Partial return maturity
- Overlapping channel audiences
- Imperfect campaign tagging
- Differences between order date and reporting date

Required validation rules should still prevent:

- Negative sessions
- Negative units
- Negative spend
- Negative inventory
- Orders exceeding sessions
- Units sold exceeding available inventory
- Duplicate primary keys
- Invalid dates
- Invalid campaign phases
- Broken transaction reconciliation beyond an accepted tolerance

---

## **17. Analytical Assumptions**

- Regression and tree-based models are used to support business conclusions rather than replace business judgment.
- Model outputs should be translated into plain-language findings.
- Feature importance does not automatically imply causation.
- Bayesian results depend on the selected prior.
- Sensitivity analysis should be used where assumptions materially affect conclusions.
- Findings should distinguish:
  - Observed facts
  - Modeled estimates
  - Business interpretations
  - Recommendations

---

## **18. Presentation Assumptions**

- The primary audience is a cross-functional executive leadership team.
- The main presentation should prioritize clarity and business implications.
- Detailed model methodology should be placed in the appendix.
- Every main slide should communicate one principal conclusion.
- Technical methods should support the narrative without dominating it.
- Uncertainty should be communicated clearly but not in unnecessarily technical language.

---

## **19. Known Limitations**

Because the case uses synthetic data:

- No result represents actual Salomon performance.
- The assumed campaign effects are built into the data-generating process.
- The analysis demonstrates methodology and reasoning rather than discovering an unknown real-world truth.
- The synthetic relationships should remain realistic enough that the analytical conclusions are not immediately obvious.
- The final presentation should disclose that the data is synthetic.
